Mapping Australia's Education and Employment Gap

This visualisation explores how education levels and employment outcomes differ across Australian states, territories, and regions.

Labour Market Overview

State and Territory Unemployment Comparison (December 2025)

This bar chart provides a broad overview of unemployment rates across Australian states and territories using the December 2025 Small Area Labour Markets data. It introduces the overall labour market differences before exploring smaller regional patterns.

The chart shows that unemployment rates differ across states and territories, with Victoria recording the highest unemployment rate and the Australian Capital Territory recording the lowest in this dataset. However, the differences between most states and territories are relatively small, suggesting that the broad state-level picture does not fully explain where unemployment pressure is concentrated.

This means that state-level comparison is useful as a starting point, but it can smooth out local differences. To better understand unemployment patterns, the analysis needs to move from broad state averages to smaller regional areas within each state.

Australia's Unemployment Trend Over Time

This line chart shows how Australia's unemployment rate changed from December 2010 to December 2025 using quarterly Small Area Labour Markets data. After comparing unemployment across states and regions, this chart adds a time-based perspective to show whether unemployment has generally increased, decreased, or remained stable.

The trend shows that unemployment has fluctuated over time rather than following one constant direction. Although the unemployment rate in December 2025 is lower than in December 2010, the pattern is not a smooth decline. There are periods where unemployment rises and falls, suggesting that labour-market conditions change over time.

The key conclusion is that unemployment should be understood through both geography and time. The state and regional charts show where unemployment differs, while this line chart shows that the national labour market also changes across different periods.

Regional Unemployment Across Australia (December 2025)

This map builds on the state-level comparison by showing unemployment rates across SA2 regions in Australia. The state filter allows users to select one state or territory at a time and explore how unemployment varies between smaller local areas.

The map shows that unemployment becomes more uneven when it is viewed at a smaller geographic level. Some SA2 regions have higher unemployment rates than nearby regions within the same state, suggesting that labour market disadvantage is often concentrated locally rather than spread evenly across the whole state.

This reveals why the state-level chart alone is not enough. A state may appear to have a moderate unemployment rate overall, but the regional map can show specific local communities with noticeably higher unemployment. Therefore, combining state-level and regional-level visualisations gives a clearer picture of labour market inequality in Australia.

Labour Force Participation and Workforce Activity

Labour Force Participation Trend in Australia

This line chart shows Australia's labour force participation rate from March 2016 to March 2026. Participation rate measures the share of people who are active in the labour market, either by working or looking for work.

The chart shows that Australia's participation rate generally increased over the period, rising from around 64.9% in March 2016 to around 66.8% in March 2026. This suggests that a larger share of the population became active in the labour market over time.

This is important because unemployment rate alone does not show the full labour market condition. A region or country can have a low unemployment rate, but if participation is also low, it may mean many people are not actively looking for work. By comparing unemployment with participation, the analysis gives a more complete view of workforce activity in Australia.

Employment Indicators by State and Territory (December 2025)

This heatmap compares multiple labour market indicators across Australian states and territories in December 2025, including unemployment rate, participation rate, employment-to-population ratio, and underemployment rate. Instead of focusing on one measure only, this graph provides a broader view of workforce activity and labour market strength.

The heatmap shows that labour market performance can look different depending on which indicator is used. A state may appear stronger when looking only at unemployment rate, but the picture can change when participation rate, employment-to-population ratio, and underemployment rate are also considered.

The key conclusion is that stronger labour market conditions are not defined by one number alone. They are better understood through a combination of lower unemployment, higher participation, higher employment-to-population ratio, and lower underemployment. This makes the heatmap useful for comparing whether a state performs consistently well across several indicators or only looks strong in one area.

Education and Qualification Profile

Bachelor Degree Attainment by State and Territory (2025)

This lollipop chart compares the proportion of people aged 15-74 years with a bachelor degree or above across Australian states and territories in 2025. It marks the beginning of the education-focused section by showing how university-level qualification attainment differs across Australia.

The chart shows a clear gap in bachelor degree attainment between states and territories. The Australian Capital Territory has the highest proportion of people with a bachelor degree or above, at 46.3%, while Tasmania has the lowest, at 27.3%. Victoria and New South Wales also record relatively high proportions compared with several other states.

This suggests that university-level education is not distributed evenly across Australia. Some states and territories have a much larger share of degree-qualified residents than others, which may reflect differences in workforce composition, access to higher education, and the concentration of professional or knowledge-based employment.

The key conclusion is that education profiles vary substantially across Australia. This matters for the wider story because differences in educational attainment may help explain why labour market outcomes also vary between places, which will be explored further in the later visualisations.

Relationship Between Education and Employment

Labour Force Status by Qualification Type (2025)

This 100% stacked bar chart compares the labour force status of Australians aged 15-74 years in 2025 across three qualification groups: people with a bachelor degree, people with a Certificate III/IV qualification, and people without a non-school qualification. Each bar shows the proportion who are employed full-time, employed part-time, unemployed, or not in the labour force.

The chart shows that people with higher qualifications are more likely to be employed full-time. Around 61.0% of people with a bachelor degree are employed full-time, compared with 57.8% of those with a Certificate III/IV qualification and only 33.0% of those without a non-school qualification.

The opposite pattern appears for people who are not in the labour force. Only 15.5% of bachelor degree holders are outside the labour force, compared with 20.2% of Certificate III/IV holders and 38.1% of people without a non-school qualification. This suggests that lower qualification levels are associated with weaker attachment to the workforce.

The key conclusion is that education level appears closely related to labour market participation and employment outcomes. People with a bachelor degree or Certificate III/IV are much more likely to be in full-time work, while people without a non-school qualification are more likely to be outside the labour force or unemployed. This strengthens the wider story that education and qualifications play an important role in shaping employment opportunities in Australia.

Industry Opportunities and Labour Demand

Industry Employment vs Median Weekly Full-time earnings

This butterfly chart compares the ten largest employing industries in Australia in 2025 with their median weekly full-time earnings. The left side shows the number of employed people in each industry, while the right side shows the median weekly earnings of full-time employees. The dashed line marks the all-industries median weekly full-time earnings of $1,741.

The chart shows that industries with the largest workforces are not always the highest-paying industries. Health Care and Social Assistance is the largest employing industry, followed by Professional, Scientific and Technical Services, Construction, Retail Trade, and Education and Training. However, their earnings levels differ substantially.

Professional, Scientific and Technical Services records median weekly full-time earnings well above the all-industries median, while Retail Trade and Accommodation and Food Services employ large numbers of people but sit noticeably below the median earnings benchmark. Health Care and Social Assistance has the largest workforce among the industries shown, yet its median weekly full-time earnings are slightly below the all-industries median.

The key conclusion is that industry size and earning potential do not always move together . Some industries provide a large number of employment opportunities but lower typical full-time earnings, while others employ fewer people yet offer higher pay. This suggests that job opportunities should be assessed not only by how many roles an industry supports, but also by the income outcomes it tends to provide.

Employment Change Across the 10 Largest Industries

The chart shows that employment growth was not evenly distributed across major industries. Health Care and Social Assistance recorded the largest absolute increase, growing by about 117,700 employed people. Professional, Scientific and Technical Services also experienced strong growth, increasing by about 87,400 workers. This suggests that service-based and knowledge-intensive industries were important drivers of employment expansion over the period.

In contrast, Manufacturing experienced a decline of around 28,500 employed people, while Accommodation and Food Services recorded a smaller fall. Construction and Retail Trade, despite being among the largest employing industries, changed only slightly between 2024 and 2025.

The key conclusion is that industry size does not necessarily indicate employment momentum. Some of Australia's largest industries are continuing to expand strongly, while others are remaining relatively stable or declining. This adds to the previous graph by showing that industry opportunities should be considered not only in terms of current workforce size and earnings, but also in terms of whether employment is growing over time.

Industry Workforce Size vs Job Vacancy Pressure (2025)

The chart shows that industries with the largest workforces do not always experience the highest vacancy pressure. Health Care and Social Assistance has by far the largest workforce and a large number of vacancies, but its vacancy pressure sits only slightly above the all-industries benchmark. In contrast, Administrative and Support Services has a much smaller workforce but the highest vacancy pressure, at around 63 vacancies per 1,000 employed people.

Accommodation and Food Services also stands out with relatively high vacancy pressure, despite employing far fewer people than Health Care and Social Assistance. This suggests that some industries may face stronger hiring demand relative to their workforce size, even if they are not among the largest employers overall.

The key conclusion is that job opportunity should not be judged only by industry size . A large workforce can indicate many existing jobs, but vacancy pressure reveals how actively an industry may still be seeking additional workers. Looking at both measures together provides a more complete view of labour demand across Australian industries.

Industry Opportunity Profile Across Earnings, Vacancy Pressure and Workforce Size (2025)

The chart shows that no single industry ranks highest across all three measures. Professional, Scientific and Technical Services ranks first for median weekly earnings and remains relatively strong in both vacancy pressure and workforce size. Health Care and Social Assistance ranks first for workforce size and also performs strongly in vacancy pressure, but it does not lead on earnings.

Other industries show clear trade-offs. Accommodation and Food Services ranks first for vacancy pressure, indicating relatively strong hiring demand compared with its workforce size, but it ranks near the bottom for median weekly earnings. Manufacturing also ranks highly for vacancy pressure, yet sits lower in workforce size and only mid-range in earnings.

The key conclusion is that industry opportunity depends on which outcome is being prioritised . Some industries stand out for pay, others for hiring demand, and others for the scale of existing employment. Looking across all three indicators together provides a more balanced view than judging industries from a single measure alone.

So, What Shapes Employment Opportunities in Australia?

Overall, the visualisations suggest that employment opportunities in Australia are shaped by a combination of location, education, and industry conditions. Unemployment and workforce participation vary across places, qualifications are associated with stronger labour force outcomes, and industries differ in their size, earnings, growth, and hiring demand. This shows that opportunity cannot be judged from one measure alone, but needs to be understood through the broader labour market context.
